# US Higher Ed for Brits

A friendly, plain-English guide to the US college and university system for anyone coming from the UK (or just curious). It explains the terms, the structure, the costs and the student journey, and lets you try registering for a semester yourself.

## Live site

Once GitHub Pages is enabled, the guide is here:

`https://<your-username>.github.io/<repo-name>/`

(Replace `<your-username>` and `<repo-name>` with your own once it's live.)

## What's inside

- **Glossary and translator** – over 100 terms with their US meaning, a "false friends" warning where the word means something different in the UK, and a plain example. Search any term or filter by category.
- **Student journey** – a visual map plus a detailed timeline from applying in high school through to graduation.
- **How it's structured** – how a university nests, the degree ladder, course numbering and prerequisites, the academic year, gen eds, community college transfers, and admissions, with links to real public course catalogs.
- **Paying for it** – how tuition ties to credit hours, the flat-rate full-time band, fees, and financial aid (scholarships, grants, loans, work-study).
- **Try it yourself** – an interactive Fall registration simulator. Build a schedule as a freshman (declared or undeclared) or a junior, with a live degree audit and feedback.
- **Pub quiz** – a 50+ question bank. Take a full mixed quiz (10, 20 or 40 questions) or drill a single topic.

## How it works

Everything lives in one self-contained file, `us-higher-ed-for-brits.html`. No build step, no dependencies, no tracking. `index.html` simply redirects to it so the site loads at the repo root.

## Hosting it yourself

1. Put `index.html` and `us-higher-ed-for-brits.html` in the repo root.
2. Push to a public GitHub repo.
3. Go to Settings → Pages, set the source to your branch and the `/ (root)` folder, and save.
4. Your site appears at the URL above within a minute or two.

## A note on accuracy

Definitions reflect typical US practice. Individual institutions vary, so treat specifics (credit thresholds, the example degree plan, admissions tests) as illustrative rather than universal.
