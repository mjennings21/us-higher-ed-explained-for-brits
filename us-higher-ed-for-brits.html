The student journey, application to graduation

Where the key decisions happen. Most confusion for UK colleagues comes from when things occur, declaring a major is not at application, and registration repeats every single term.

How students actually choose where to apply

Myth: every US student fires off applications to a dozen universities. Reality: plenty apply to only a few, or even one. The selective, apply-everywhere scramble is mostly a top-end story.

A very common, practical approach is:

1. Does it offer my degree?
Students look for schools that actually run the major or program they want.
2. What will it cost?
Tuition, in-state vs out-of-state pricing, and the financial aid on offer weigh heavily. Cost often decides it.
3. Can I get in, and is it close?
Many choose a nearby state school with accessible entry requirements, sometimes living at home to save money.

The basic entry requirement

To enroll at a college or university you normally need a high school diploma or a GED (the equivalent for those who didn't finish high school). Beyond that, requirements range from open admission (a pulse and a diploma will do, common at community colleges and many state schools) up to the highly selective privates that reject most applicants and may require SAT or ACT scores.

How choosing classes actually works

Every term, a student opens the course catalog and builds their own timetable. They don't just get assigned classes. They pick the courses they need, and often the specific section, which means choosing the professor and the time slot that fits around their other classes, a job, or commuting.

Each course is offered in one or more formats:

In person on campus
Traditional classroom or lab sessions at a set time and place.
Online remote
Delivered over the internet. Can be synchronous (live at a set time) or asynchronous (self-paced, watch recordings on your own schedule).
Hybrid a mix
Part in person, part online, e.g. a weekly in-person seminar plus online lectures.

Common misconception: people assume a student is either "an online student" or "an in-person student". Most are neither. A single student routinely mixes all three formats in one term, choosing each course's format to fit their schedule.

What order classes are taken in

Courses are numbered by level, which tells you the difficulty and roughly when you take them. The first number is the giveaway:

100-level e.g. Biology 101
Introductory, no prior knowledge assumed. Mostly taken by freshmen. "101" means the entry-level first course in a subject.
200-level e.g. Biology 201
Builds on the basics. Typically sophomore year, often with a 100-level prerequisite.
300 and 400-level
Advanced, specialised courses for juniors and seniors, usually with several prerequisites behind them.

Prerequisites enforce this order: you must pass the earlier course before you can register for the next. That's why a student can't just jump into an advanced class, they have to climb the ladder. (Numbering schemes vary, some schools use 1000 and 2000 instead of 100 and 200.)

The academic year, and the summer term

The common rhythm is two main semesters, fall (Aug to Dec) and spring (Jan to May), plus an optional summer session.

Fall & spring ~15 weeks each
The standard full-length terms most students follow.
Summer session shorter and intensive
Compressed into fewer weeks, so the same material is covered faster. Optional, and not everyone takes it.

Students use summer to graduate faster by going "straight through" the year, to catch up, or to retake a course they failed or want a better grade in. (Schools on the quarter system run fall, winter and spring quarters plus an optional summer instead.)

Two kinds of institution Brits often merge

4-year college / university bachelor's degree
The "default" route. Public (state-funded, e.g. UCLA) or private (e.g. Stanford). "College" and "university" are used interchangeably by students.
Community college 2-year, associate degree
Local, low-cost, open access. Many students do two years here then "transfer" into a 4-year school to finish a bachelor's. No UK sixth-form equivalent, it is post-18 higher ed.

The common money-saving pathway

A huge number of US students deliberately start at a community college to save money. Tuition there is far cheaper per credit, so they take their general-education courses (the broad first-two-year requirements) at the community college, then transfer into a 4-year university to complete their major. The bachelor's degree is awarded by the university, and the diploma looks the same. This route doesn't exist in the UK and surprises many British colleagues.

Getting a head start in high school

US students can begin earning college credit before they even leave high school, two routes worth knowing:

Dual enrollment
A high-schooler takes real college courses for genuine college credit while still in high school, sometimes at a nearby community college.
AP (Advanced Placement)
College-level courses and exams taken in high school. A strong exam score can earn college credit or let a student skip introductory courses.

Both mean a student can arrive at university already part-way through their requirements, sometimes a full semester ahead.

Getting in: admissions at a glance

The baseline to enroll anywhere is a high school diploma or a GED (the equivalent for those who didn't finish high school). Above that baseline, selectivity is a wide spectrum, not one bar:

Open admission community colleges, many state schools
Almost anyone with a diploma or GED is accepted. Getting in is easy; the challenge is finishing.
Selective most universities
Weigh GPA, essays, activities and often SAT or ACT scores. A genuine application, but most qualified applicants have options.
Highly selective elite privates, top flagships
Reject most applicants. Many reinstated required SAT/ACT scores for 2025-2026.

The two main tests

The SAT (digital, scored to 1600, no separate science section) and the ACT (scored 1 to 36, with a science section that became optional in 2025) are interchangeable, colleges accept either. Students take whichever suits them. There is no UK equivalent; UK admissions lean on A-level grades instead.

How many schools students apply to

Despite the stereotype, not everyone applies to a dozen places. Many apply to just a few, or one, picked on a practical basis: which schools offer their degree, what it will cost, and whether a nearby state school will take them. The apply-everywhere race is mostly a top-end phenomenon.

How tuition is actually billed

Tuition (the price of instruction) is usually tied to credit hours, the unit that measures course workload. A typical course is 3 credits, and a bachelor's needs about 120 credits to graduate. How those credits are priced depends on the student's load:

Part-time below the full-time line, often under 12 credits
Charged per credit hour. Take fewer courses, pay less. Each extra course adds cost.
Full-time band at many schools, roughly 12 to 18 credits
Charged a single flat rate. This is the surprising part: within this band, adding another course costs nothing extra. A student taking 15 credits and one taking 18 often pay the same tuition.
Overload above the cap, e.g. more than 18 credits
Extra courses beyond the band usually trigger an overload fee per additional credit, and may need approval.

Why it matters: a motivated full-time student can load up on courses inside the flat-rate band to graduate faster or explore more subjects at no extra tuition. The exact thresholds vary by institution, so treat 12 and 18 as typical, not universal.

A perk of the flat-rate band: fun electives

Because a full-time student inside the flat-rate band pays the same tuition whether they take, say, 12 or 16 credits, adding an extra elective often costs little more than that course's student fees. The tuition is already covered.

So students use electives to try things cheaply that would be expensive outside school: scuba diving, tennis, ice hockey, social dance, photography, cooking and the like. They also break up a heavy load of hard major courses with something enjoyable. It's one of the genuinely nice quirks of the US system, and a useful thing to understand about how students actually fill their schedules.

The full cost is more than tuition

Tuition
The price of instruction. Varies hugely: private schools cost more, and public schools charge in-state residents far less than out-of-state students.
Student fees
Mandatory charges on top of tuition for things like technology, the health centre, student activities, athletics and facilities. Often billed per term regardless of credit load.
Room and board
Housing ("room") and a meal plan ("board") for students living on campus, billed separately from tuition.
Other costs
Books, supplies, transport and personal expenses. Schools publish a total "cost of attendance" that bundles all of this.

How students bring the price down: financial aid

The "sticker price" is rarely what a student actually pays. Financial aid is the package that reduces it, and it's central to how Americans choose a school. It starts with the FAFSA (Free Application for Federal Student Aid), the form that determines eligibility for federal support. Aid comes in four main types:

Scholarships do not repay
Usually merit-based, awarded for achievement (academic, athletic, artistic). Come from the school, private organisations or companies.
Grants do not repay
Usually need-based, awarded according to financial need and income. Federal (e.g. Pell Grant), state or institutional.
Student loans repaid later
Borrowed money. Federal loans have set terms; private loans behave more like ordinary debt. Unlike the UK's salary-linked system, US loans are repaid as conventional debt.
Work-study earned
A federal program giving students with financial need part-time campus jobs to help cover costs.

Quick distinction to remember: scholarships and grants are free money you keep (merit vs need), loans are borrowed and repaid, and work-study is earned through a job. A student's "aid package" is usually a blend of all four.
